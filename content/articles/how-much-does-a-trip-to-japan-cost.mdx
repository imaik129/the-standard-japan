**A 10-day trip to Japan costs between ¥150,000 and ¥350,000 per person.** That is roughly $1,000 to $2,300 USD at current rates — before flights. Whether you land closer to the low end or the high end depends almost entirely on three decisions: where you sleep, how you get between cities, and how much you eat at restaurants versus convenience stores.

Japan is not a cheap destination. But it is a *fair* one. The costs are transparent, there are no scams, and the value you get — even at the mid-range — is exceptional. Here is exactly what to budget, line by line.

---

## The Fast Answer: Japan Daily Budget by Travel Style

| Travel Style | Daily Budget (per person) |
|---|---|
| Budget (hostels, convenience stores) | ¥8,000–¥12,000 |
| Mid-range (business hotels, local restaurants) | ¥15,000–¥25,000 |
| Comfort (nice hotels, izakayas, day trips) | ¥30,000–¥50,000 |
| Luxury (boutique ryokan, omakase, private) | ¥70,000+ |

Most visitors spending two weeks in Japan land somewhere between mid-range and comfort: ¥20,000–¥35,000 per day, all in.

## Accommodation Costs in Japan

This is where your budget swings most dramatically.

**Hostels and capsule hotels (¥2,500–¥5,000/night):** Capsule hotels are not a compromise — they are a genuinely good sleep at a low price. Book Millennials Shibuya, Nine Hours Shinjuku, or The Lect Osaka. Clean, quiet, great locations.

**Business hotels (¥8,000–¥15,000/night):** The sweet spot for most travelers. Toyoko Inn, Dormy Inn, and APA Hotel are reliable chains with small but comfortable rooms and excellent locations near train stations. Breakfast is often included or cheap.

**Boutique and design hotels (¥20,000–¥40,000/night):** Brands like Trunk Hotel, Andaz Tokyo, and Hotel Canopy by Hilton Ginza offer genuine design and style without the full luxury price tag.

**Ryokan (¥30,000–¥100,000+/night per person):** A traditional inn with kaiseki dinner and breakfast included. Non-negotiable for at least one night if this is your first visit to Japan. Prices vary enormously — you can find good mid-tier ryokan in Hakone or Kyoto for ¥20,000–¥30,000 per person.

## Transportation Costs: The Japan Rail Pass Question

This question comes up constantly. **The JR Pass is only worth it if you're covering significant distances.** Here is how to calculate:

**JR Pass prices (2026):**
- 7-day: ¥50,000
- 14-day: ¥80,000
- 21-day: ¥100,000

A single Tokyo–Kyoto Shinkansen round trip costs about ¥28,000. Add Hiroshima or Osaka and the 7-day pass easily pays off for a Tokyo–Kansai trip.

If you are staying in Tokyo for most of your trip, skip the pass. An IC card (Suica or Pasmo) loaded with ¥5,000–¥10,000 covers all local trains, subways, and buses in any major city.

**Within-city transportation:**
- Single subway/train ride: ¥170–¥300
- IC card top-up per week in a major city: ¥2,000–¥4,000
- Taxi: expensive. Use trains.

## Food Costs in Japan

Japan has the most stratified and consistent food pricing of any country in the world. You know exactly what you are getting at every price point.

**¥500–¥1,000 per meal:** Convenience store meals (genuinely excellent — do not skip this), ramen chains, standing soba, gyudon chains like Yoshinoya and Matsuya. This is real Japanese food, not budget food.

**¥1,000–¥2,500 per meal:** The main range for local restaurants, izakayas for lunch, ramen shops, sushi conveyor belts, tonkatsu sets. This is where most of Japan's best food lives.

**¥3,000–¥8,000 per meal:** Good izakayas for dinner with drinks, kaiseki lunch, mid-tier sushi.

**¥10,000–¥30,000+ per meal:** Omakase sushi, high kaiseki, Michelin restaurants.

**Realistic daily food budget:** ¥3,000–¥6,000 covers three solid meals if you mix convenience stores with local restaurants. ¥8,000–¥12,000 if you eat at sit-down restaurants every meal.

## Activities and Entrance Fees

Japan does not bleed you on attractions. Most temples and shrines are free or ¥500–¥1,000 to enter.

**Common costs:**
- Major temple entrance (Kinkakuji, Fushimi Inari): free – ¥1,000
- TeamLab borderless/Planets: ¥3,200–¥4,000
- DisneySea / Disneyland: ¥10,900
- Tokyo Skytree: ¥2,100–¥3,400
- Sumo tournament (ballpark seat): ¥3,800
- Day trips (Hakone, Nikko): ¥3,000–¥8,000 all-in with transport

Budget ¥2,000–¥5,000 per day for activities if you are doing something every day.

---

## Shopping: The Budget Wildcard

Clothing, ceramics, stationery, whisky, electronics. Japan is dangerous for shoppers.

Set a hard shopping budget before you arrive. ¥20,000–¥50,000 is realistic for most visitors. If you are buying electronics, cameras, or luxury goods in Ginza, budget separately.

The yen has been weak against the dollar and euro since 2022. As of mid-2026, international visitors are still getting approximately 15–20% more purchasing power than in 2019. That will not last forever — it is a genuine advantage right now.

## FAQ: Japan Trip Cost

**Is Japan expensive compared to other Asian countries?**
Yes — Japan is significantly more expensive than Southeast Asia, and comparable to Western Europe. But the quality-to-price ratio is among the highest in the world.

**How much cash should I bring to Japan?**
Carry at least ¥20,000–¥30,000 in cash at all times. Many restaurants, small shops, and vending machines are still cash-only, though IC cards and credit cards have become more accepted since 2023.

**Can I do Japan on ¥5,000 a day?**
Technically yes, if you sleep in the cheapest hostels and eat only convenience store food. But this is not a satisfying way to experience Japan and we do not recommend it.

**Is the JR Pass still worth it in 2026?**
Only if you plan to travel between multiple cities. The JR Pass prices increased significantly in 2023. Run the numbers on Hyperdia or Google Maps before buying.

**How much does a ryokan cost?**
Entry-level ryokan start around ¥15,000–¥20,000 per person per night, including dinner and breakfast. Mid-tier ryokan in Hakone or Arima Onsen run ¥25,000–¥50,000. High-end can exceed ¥100,000 per person.

**What is the cheapest month to visit Japan?**
January–February (excluding New Year's week) and June (rainy season) offer the lowest hotel prices. Cherry blossom season (late March–April) and Golden Week (late April–early May) are the most expensive.

**Do I need travel insurance for Japan?**
Yes. Medical costs in Japan are high for uninsured foreign visitors. Budget around ¥5,000–¥15,000 for a two-week policy depending on your coverage level and home country.

**How much does it cost to eat sushi in Japan?**
A conveyor belt sushi lunch (kaiten-zushi) costs ¥1,500–¥3,000. A good neighborhood sushi restaurant runs ¥3,000–¥8,000 for dinner. A proper omakase experience starts at ¥15,000 and can exceed ¥50,000 per person.
